Crafting descriptive titles:
Titles are short, factual descriptions of your content. Your titles can impact buyers’ decisions to license your content, so it’s important to craft them with care. 

Here are some dos and don’ts:
Do: Use titles to introduce your content to customers. Titles are searchable.
Do: Keep titles accurate, relevant, descriptive, and precise. 
Do: Limit titles to 70 characters or fewer to help customers find your content via web search results. 
Don’t: Use formal sentence structures when creating titles. Customers just need phrases that are easy to read and understand. 

Crafting descriptions
Descriptions are slightly longer and more complete than titles, but still factual descriptions of your content. Your descriptions can impact buyers’ decisions to license your content.
Limit descriptions to 200 characters or fewer to help customers find your content.
All the other description features should observe the same guidelines as the titles.

Creating effective keywords:
Here’s what you need to know to create keywords that will help potential buyers find your content:
Keyword order is critical: Arrange keywords in order of importance. This is most critical thing you can do to help ensure that buyers can find your content on Adobe Stock.
Keyword order matters, with the first 10 affecting search results the most.
Be descriptive but not verbose: You may enter from 45 to 49 keywords - single-word terms that are accurate, relevant, and aligned with current trends in stock photography. Different content types require different levels of keyword complexity. Find strategy that allows you to tell the story consistently, but be sure to consider adding the following information to your metadata:
•	Who or what is the subject?
•	What actions are being depicted?
•	What’s the setting for the subject? 
•	Are there key compositional or technical aspects to your content?
•	Are there words that you can use to highlight specific and unique details in your assets?
Bring words from the title into your keywords: Use the title you’ve created as starting point for your keywords. Include the individual words and concepts from your title in your top 10 keywords for extra bump in search relevance.

Follow these additional guidelines:
• Separate keywords: Separate descriptive elements from subjects. For example, “white,” “fluffy,” “young animal,” and “pup” are accurate, but you should add them as separate keywords. If you combine them into one keyword, it won’t be translated or surfaced correctly to buyers. 
• Multi-word phrases are to be avoided in keywords to maintain simplicity and effectiveness in metadata.
• Conceptual elements: Add conceptual keywords that describe feelings, mood, or trends (e.g., solitude, childhood, milestones, and conservation). “Cold” is appropriate keyword for image of ice cube — “heat” isn’t. 
• Describe the setting: Use keywords like “indoors,” “outdoors,” “day,” “night,” “sunny,” or “cloudy.” 
• Viewpoint: Include viewpoint depicted in picture. For example, “high-angle view,” “directly above,” “aerial view,” or “drone point of view.” 
• Demographic information: If you're aware of information such as ethnicity, race, heritage, age, gender, or disability, include these specifics in the keywords.
• Nouns should be singular. For example, use “cat” instead of “cats.”
• Simple is better—be descriptive, not verbose.
